# WEIZAC: Weizmann Automatic Computer

## Overview
WEIZAC (Weizmann Automatic Computer) was the first computer in Israel and one of the first
large-scale, stored-program, electronic computers in the world. It was built at the
Weizmann Institute of Science in Rehovot, Israel during 1954-1955, based on the IAS
(Institute for Advanced Study) architecture developed by John von Neumann.

It was operational from late 1955 until December 29, 1963. Until 1961, WEIZAC was the
only computer in the State of Israel.

As with almost all computers of its era, it was a one-of-a-kind machine that could not
exchange programs with other IAS-derivative computers.

## Key People

- **Chaim L. Pekeris** - Initiated the project. Worked at IAS during von Neumann machine design.
  Wanted it to solve Laplace's tidal equations. Established Dept. of Applied Mathematics.
- **Chaim Weizmann** - Israel's first president, head of Weizmann Institute. Funded the project.
- **John von Neumann** - Supported the plan. When asked "What will that tiny country do with
  an electric computer?" he responded: "Don't worry. If nobody else uses it, Pekeris will
  use it full time!"
- **Albert Einstein** - Advisory committee member. Initially skeptical, eventually approved.
- **Gerald Estrin** - Research engineer from the von Neumann project at Princeton. Led the
  WEIZAC construction. Brought schematics (but no parts) from Princeton to Israel.
- **Thelma Estrin** - Gerald's wife, electrical engineer, also involved in the project.
- **Ephraim Frei** - Israeli physicist, recruited from Princeton.
- **Micha Kedem** - Israeli electrical engineer, helped source parts (later joined IBM Israel).
- **Zvi Riesel** - Former head of IDF radar workshop, saw newspaper ad for the project.
- **Phillip Rabinowitz** - Had worked on the Whirlwind Computer project. Gave Israel's first
  programming courses at Weizmann Institute.
- **Hans Jarosch** - Ph.D. Aeronautical Engineering, wrote application programs.
- **Yigal Accad** - Programmed Pekeris' equations for two-electron atom quantum system.

## Specifications

| Feature | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Architecture | IAS (von Neumann) derivative |
| Word size | 40 bits |
| Instruction format | 20 bits: 8-bit opcode + 12-bit address |
| Instructions per word | 2 (left and right halves) |
| Operation | Asynchronous (no central clock) |
| I/O | Punched paper tape (initially); magnetic tape (from 1958) |
| Initial memory | 1,024 words magnetic drum |
| Upgraded memory | 4,096 words magnetic-core memory |
| Final memory | 12,288 words (3 x 4,096-word modules, from 1961) |
| Negative numbers | Two's complement |
| Registers | Accumulator (AC), Multiplier/Quotient (MQ) |

## Scientific Achievements

WEIZAC was used for groundbreaking calculations:

1. **Ocean Tides**: Solved Laplace's tidal equations. Discovered an amphidromic point in
   the South Atlantic where the tide does not change.
2. **Quantum Mechanics**: Numerically calculated eigenvalues of a two-electron atom quantum
   system (programmed by Yigal Accad from Pekeris' equations). Results were later
   experimentally verified by Brookhaven National Laboratory, confirming the correctness
   of Schrodinger's Equations.
3. **Other problems**: Earthquakes, atomic spectroscopy, X-ray crystallography, random walk
   methods, numerical analysis.

## The Story

The project reads like a fairy tale. Post-War of Independence Israel was being built from
scratch, lacking housing, roads, infrastructure, absorbing hundreds of thousands of
immigrants. Yet Weizmann had the audacity to spend 20% of the Institute's budget on a
computer.

Gerald Estrin arrived on a ship carrying immigrants from North Africa. He brought schematics
but no parts. To recruit staff, a newspaper ad was posted. Most applicants had no records of
prior education because those were lost in the Holocaust or during immigration.

When precision machinery was needed, they improvised with a shop that produced fans and
bicycle parts, owned by Bulgarian immigrants. Micha Kedem and Pekeris roamed Tel Aviv
streets searching for parts.

The WEIZAC project provided an opportunity for mathematicians and engineers to move to
Israel without sacrificing their professional careers. Its success led to recognition of the
demand for computers in Israel, and ultimately provided the foundation for Israel's computer
and technology industries.

## Legacy

- WEIZAC's success created Israel's first crop of computer experts
- Led to construction of GOLEM (based on ILLIAC II architecture)
- Provided the foundation for Israel's technology industry
- IEEE Milestone in Electrical Engineering and Computing (2006)
